## Q1. The Origin

> Tell me the full story of how you got into AI video. Not just "I started using Midjourney", what were you doing before, what was the first thing you generated that made you go "holy shit," and at what point did you realize people would pay real money for this?

I started with Veo 2. The moment that I really realized, like, holy shit, this went from a cool art project to actual ads / videos that people will actually pay for, was when Google Veo 3 released. I realized the way that this tech is moving, it's going to be big.

At this point I was already a big geek inside the AI space. I had been using AI since GPT-2 came out, while I was in my last few years of high school. I was very dyslexic and I hated it, I couldn't write, I couldn't barely spell. So I instructed AI, before anyone knew what AI was, to help me write my essays in my own writing style. That's how I kind of got into it.

I started in LLMs and then I started playing inside the OpenAI playground to build my own personal homework tool. From there it led into video.

I was a film student, so I was already very comfortable telling a story and making cool videos, and I was comfortable with editing software. So for me, instead of going out to film, I could just generate a bunch of clips and let my imagination run wild.

## Q2. The Workflow

> Walk me through your actual process from start to finish. A client says "I want a video." What happens next? Every tool, every decision, every step. And where do most people's processes fall apart compared to yours?

A lot of people try to make AI video without having a good understanding of how to make a good video. **That's what people are missing.** The best place to learn how to use AI tools is actually film school, because it teaches you how to make a good video. It teaches the cinematic language, the rules of framing, the rules of editing, and how *not* to.

When a client says "I want a video," our process:

1. **Onboard them**, understand the project and produce a director's treatment. Take the script and process it.
2. **Open a project file** inside our generating infrastructure. We save context, transcripts with the client, and every piece of information we can get our hands on into that folder so our AI agents have access to it.
3. **Shooting day (virtual)**, we generate a bunch of different video and images, exactly as if we were shooting in real life.
4. **Post-production**, we post-produce that footage exactly the same way we'd post-produce normal footage.
5. **Color grading**, we spend a lot of time here, making it really look realistic.
6. **Sound design**, we spend a lot of time here too. **Sound design really makes AI visuals come to life.**

## Q3. Amateur vs Premium

> What separates the stuff that looks like a tech demo from the stuff that looks like a real production?

The biggest thing is **lighting and color realism**. A shot can look very realistic but something is off, usually it's the lighting or the colors.

AI has a tendency to fall back into the same color patterns repeatedly. You have to *force* it out. Force those cinematic colors, that cinematic grain, that cinematic framing. Force it out of its AI norms.

**AI loves to go to the median.** It loves to make a shot that the training data likes. You have to force it into higher-end, higher-quality images. When I say higher quality, I don't mean higher resolution, I mean more taste. Very cool framing.

You're using AI, literally anything is possible. So instead of conforming to normal stuff, do something crazy, something that grabs attention.

The thing that makes AI content good is whether you can tell a story so vivid and so captivating that the person watching forgets it's AI, or doesn't realize, because they're so lost in the story.

## Q4. The Tools

> What's in your stack right now and why? What did you use 6 months ago that you've dropped? What's overhyped? What's underrated?

**Most underrated:** agents. Use them to automate more and more of your workflow. The only thing you should control is the taste. If you automate everything else, it lets you focus on what's important, the creativity. Let everything else handle itself.

**Current stack:**
- **Nano Banana Pro**, image generation. Still by far the best image model out there for cinematic results.
- **Seedance**, video model. Hyped by everyone, including us, but it's the one we use.
- **Claude**, the brain behind everything. We built agents around Claude, built our own infrastructure, call all the APIs ourselves.

**Overhyped:** ChatGPT image is way overrated for this kind of production.

How we structure it: we take all the project files, let Claude process them so it has full context to work from. When I describe a scene, I close my eyes and start imagining it in my head, then describe everything in as much detail as I can, the kind of shots, where the lighting is hitting, what we're capturing, how the camera is moving.

## Q5. Hard Truths

> What are the biggest misconceptions about AI video right now?

Something that people don't talk about: **the majority of the population frowns on AI.** Inherently, in human nature, if people perceive something as easy, they value it less.

When AI came along and made things easier, it did two things:
1. Opened the doors for creatives to make whatever they want.
2. Made people value it less.

The amount of times I've been told "Oh, AI is so easy. You just press two buttons and beautiful cinematic video pops out the other end." I wish that's how it worked. It really doesn't. You need to really try.

The hard truth: to make a good video, it takes a lot of effort, talent, and skill. The general public doesn't value AI content highly because they perceive it as easy. A lot of people just learn how to prompt, learn what the tools are called, start making slop, and call themselves creatives. In reality, it still takes a lot of knowledge to make a good video and edit something that tells a story.

You won't be able to buy any course that's telling you you're behind on AI. They won't be able to teach you anything that's going to help you create good videos if you don't have the base knowledge of creating good content down.

## Q6. The Business

> How do you price this? What makes a video worth $5K–$10K? Who's your ideal client?

We price higher because we deliver quality that's completely unrivalled. We have **40 years of filmmaking experience between us**, and that shows up in the videos we make.

A lot of people use AI to cut costs. **We do the opposite: we use AI so we can do what we imagine.** Projects we imagine would cost millions of dollars to do traditionally, which is just not feasible.

For example, if a brand wants something completely beautiful and out of this world, crazy set design, outfits, locations, camera movements that would cost hundreds of thousands of dollars, if not millions, most brands can't afford that. We do it for a fraction of the price and at multiples of the speed, while still having that taste, quality, and story behind it.

Another hard truth: **AI content will not convert for brands if it's used to cut costs.** People will not buy into it. People will feel disingenuous. They can always tell when you're using AI to cut costs vs. when you're using AI because it's the best tool available to execute your vision.

## Q7. War Stories

> Tell me about a project that went sideways.

About two months ago there was a massive issue with Google servers. The majority of providers for Nano Banana, the primary image model that most AI creatives use because it's the best one right now, were suffering severe downstream issues. Their platforms weren't working, which caused us to basically eat shit with all our clients because we couldn't create work and we had deadlines.

We had to make a full video in 20 hours, which was literally impossible. We needed a week on it but couldn't work during the week because the platform was offline. We were getting screwed.

**How we handled it:** got a bigger team together, split it up into chunks, and did the whole thing in one day.

When generations aren't working, often the AI just won't listen correctly. Usually you have to try a different approach, same thing but different words, different references. People are often surprised by how a tiny detail in your reference images can affect the final output. These tools rely on context alone to generate good results, so you really have to make sure you're feeding it the best quality stuff you can.

**That's why we spend a lot of pre-production time** collecting and creating references, and getting client approval on those elements.

## Q8. The Future

> Where is this going in the next year? Is AI going to replace traditional videographers?

When the AI hype dies down a bit, people are going to realise and accept that **AI is just another tool** that creative people can use to tell their stories more effectively, without limits.

The important thing for me is to empower creatives to create without limits, without the limitations of budget, timing, or location. AI has made it possible for the best idea to win. **It's no longer about who has the most money or who could hire the best people. It's about who has the best idea.**

I don't think AI will replace traditional videography entirely. There will always be event videography, there will always be stuff to record in person. But I feel the content ecosystem will take a big hit once we can mimic humans very effectively.

In the future I see agents automating very large chunks of the process. We're going to have an epidemic of companies using agents to create content, and it's all going to feel and look the same. **The only way to avoid that is by keeping humans in the loop.**

> **My slogan: "taste can't be automated, but everything else can."**

The reason taste can't be automated is that LLMs will never be able to understand what makes us *us*. There are tools getting closer, but for the foreseeable future humans always need to be involved.

Something that makes feature films so good and so emotional is the *environment* they're made in, people bouncing ideas off each other, that culture on set, 50 people slaving away for 15 hours a day, getting tired, joking, building friendships, building community, putting all that emotion into the videos. **That's what makes them so good. Without that environment you can't make videos that good.**

There will never be a solo good film. No one can solo a good film. You need that environment, you need to bounce off people. Humans are herd animals; humanity is community. That's going to apply in AI filmmaking too.

## Q9. Starting From Zero

> If someone wants to get into AI video as a business tomorrow, what should they do first?

Weird plug, but I'm going to be opening my doors to do an **internship / mentorship programme for free in one or two months**. It's free, so it would be nice to get a few people in. We can sell info products afterwards.

What I'd recommend: the best place to learn how to make good AI videos is **film school**, because there you learn the story skills first, you lead with the right principles. Of course you need technical skills, but you also need storytelling skills, which are harder to develop. I don't know the best ways to develop storytelling skills, probably by intentionally consuming content, breaking it down, thinking about it, juxtaposing stuff.

Two really good ways to learn AI:
1. **Just use the fucking tools every single day.** You'll get better by playing with them, pushing limits, figuring out what they can do.
2. **Go to film school** and learn how to make a good video.

## Q10. The Craft Nobody Sees

> What's a technique you use that most people in this space don't?

The most important part in AI is **pre-production.** Make sure you have everything laid out, references, elements organised, etc.

One step most people skip: **having your folders structured correctly** so AI can read through and use them, and **having a lot of project context** because that really helps it write prompts.

If you have good structure and the project has a lot of context, it makes it easier to rapid-fire at the start, get yourself off the ground quickly, which makes the entire project easier.

## Q11. AI Video vs Traditional

> Someone says "just hire a videographer." What's your response?

It's not a competition. **They're both tools.** People need to realise AI is not some evil tool trying to steal everyone's jobs. AI is a tool, and if used correctly by a talented individual, it's just as powerful as having a camera in their hands.

There's no competition between AI video and traditional video. **One of the best use cases for AI video is to use it in hybrid with traditional video.**

Obviously some things AI can't generate, live events. You can't AI-generate events. But you can use AI to enhance an event video significantly.

There are also things AI is *better* at, like creating a phone UI. There it's better to use AI because it's more likely to give you the result you want.
